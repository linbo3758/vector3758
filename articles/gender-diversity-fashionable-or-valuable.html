Gender Diversity is fashionable, but is it valuable?

在时尚与价值之间：性别多样性的全球图景

In the last couple of decades in many western countries, there has been growing awareness of gender diversity 1 —(refers to variations in gender identity and expression or role rather than traditional expectations of female or male. Examples of gender diverse people include transgender people, genderqueers and non-binary people2. This is different from acknowledging that gender is a 2-category, exclusive, binary (either born a boy or a girl), and what we are more interested in is that gender is assumed to line up with our assigned sex. However, there is growing body of evidence that suggests gender might be more than just what we make it to be. The 2010s has seen the use of the word LGBTQ, and other variant. LGBTQ+ is an initial that stands for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender, plus means other LGBTQ identities and expressions other than those listed above.

Current Global Landscape of Gender Diversity

In 2025 9% of adults worldwide identify as LGBTQ from new global survey. The rate varies from 15% in Brazil to 3% in South Korea (NBCNEWS). Gallup also surveyed adults in the United States where 9.3% identify as LGBT, The UK has reported 3.3% in 2023 (ONS) but this may be rising as attitudes towards diversity in sexual identity are changing.

Legal recognition of same-sex marriage is one measure of acceptance. As things stand in 2025, 38 countries recognize same-sex marriage legally. In 20013 The Netherlands became the first country to legalise same-sex marriage, followed by Belgium in 2003 and Spain in 2005. In 2015 the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that all states must issue marriage licenses to same-sex couples4. The Marriage Act 2013 came into force in 2014 in England and Wales. China hasn't legalised same-sex marriage. What is worrying is that same sexual activity is illegal and even punishable by death in over 60 countries.

Social attitudes and legal responses continue to diverge. This April the U.K. Supreme Court defined a woman as a person born biologically female. It is the culmination of a decades-long legal battle in Scotland, England and Wales. Gender identity is deeply political in the U.S. Meanwhile, in China, BiliBili among other social medias is using TA in place of He or She, reflecting a gradual shift in the world's most populous country. Such differences show an uneven and evolving global landscape, molded by law, culture and generational factors.

Global Evolution of Gender Diversity

Gender diversity is not a new or contemporary phenomenon; it has ancient roots all over the world. Gender diversity in the United States was built upon the Indigenous "Two-Spirit" traditions5. In China Daoist philosophy has long viewed gender as fluid, and eunuchs have been vital for court in imperial China for thousands of years despite their gender ambiguous presentation. Gay or bisexual characters can be found in literary works like Dream of the Red Chamber. Roberta Cowell —first British trans woman 1951 and Christine Jorgensen —public transition 1952 are foundational moments in transgender history. The 1969 Stonewall Riots are marked as the beginning of the modern LGBTQ+ rights movement in the U.S. However, the recent spate of "bathroom bills" reveals a polarized society around issues of gender identity.

In academia, Simone de Beauvoir wrote that gender is different from biological sex6 in 1949, the foundation for the modern gender theory. In 1990, Judith Butler published her theory of gender performativity7. From the 2000s, "non-binary" and "genderqueer" start to be used more recently and was popularized even more by the social medias in the 2010s8. Today, more and more countries have included gender identity in their laws and policies.

The Value of Gender Diversity

3.1 Social Significance

3.1.1 Addressing Gender Preference and Cultural Injustice

In some patriarchal economies of pre-industrial societies, the preference for males over females has existed for a long time, and some people today still prefer to have a son, such as Bangladesh, India, China, etc. For example, due to the influence of ancient Confucianism, there are many sex-selective abortions in China today. Since the 1980s, the sex ratio of boys to girls at birth has been seriously skewed (in 2004, it peaked at 121.18, and in some counties, it reached 177) 9. Behind all these cold statistics, lie millions of baby girls yet to be born and the violation of women's reproductive rights 10.

Celebrating gender diversity — By popularizing the fact that gender is not the same as sex, we can break down patriarchal mindset and celebrate the dignity of every person, irrespective of his or her gender.

3.1.2 Mitigating Surplus Male-Driven Violence

The effects of gender imbalance go beyond demography. In China, a generation of "bare branches"—men who are unable to find partners as a result of the skewed sex ratio at birth—accounted for the 2010s. Today, over 30 million men of marrying age are unable to find a partner. The surplus, in turn, drives higher rates of gender-based violence, crime, and the trafficking of women and girls into forced marriages and sexual slavery, particularly in rural areas11.

Vicious cycles of violence are built on the gender inequality and preference. Promoting gender diversity, along with legal reform and inclusive education, will be the path forward to breaking the cycle by eradicate the causes of gender-based injustice.

3.1.3 Promoting Inclusion, Well-being, and Democratic Values

Gender diversity leads to social inclusion and good mental health by decreasing stigma, discrimination and enhancing acceptance. There is evidence that more inclusive societies have lower rates of depression and suicide, and fewer hate crimes; positive, inclusive schools and work places lead to greater belonging and mental health12 13. Educational policies that recognize and respect gender differences enable people to be themselves and treat others equally. Countries that recognize gender minorities tend to be more democratic and rights-friendly places.

3.2 Medical Significance

3.2.1 Advancing Scientific Understanding of Mechanism of Gender formation

Gender diversity- as it was once overlooked or pathologized- is now increasingly regarded as a nature element of human biology. This growing understanding among scientists has inspired a new biomedical and psychological movement dedicated to discovering the biological basis gender orientation.

Growing scientific interest has deepened our understanding that sexual orientation is a biologically trait but not a personal choice. The fraternal birth order effect14 reveals that maternal immune response to Y chromosome may cause sons to be differently sexual differentiated. Epigenetic studies have found that intra-utero environment may cause different gene expression in even twins, which results in their sexual orientation15. Meanwhile, neuroanatomical studies have found that there are differences in the structures of homosexual and heterosexual human brains16, as well as patterns of brain activity in transgender people related to body perception and gender identification17. These studies have proven that sexual minorities are not deviant, but that the sexual orientation of sexual minorities or gender identity of transgender people originates from biological diversity.

3.2.2. Progress in Mental Health Theory and Practice

LGBT individuals experience stigma, discrimination, and marginalization. Minority stress theory 12 has guided research on sexual and gender minority health and well-being. Affirmative therapy for LGBT youth 18 resulting in positive outcomes.

3.2.3. Public Health Contributions

Gender diversity has also improved public health practices, such as tailoring HIV prevention, hormone therapy, and reproductive health services. Additionally, it has fostered interdisciplinary collaboration across psychology, medicine, ethics, sociology, politics, and education, to better understand gender and sexuality.

3.3 Economic and Technological Value

3.3.1 Inclusive Economies Perform Better

There is increasing evidence that greater social inclusion of LGBT people contributes to economic performance. In 39 emerging countries, for every 1% increase in the LGBT Legal Rights Index, GDP per capita increases by $2,000 and the Human Development Index 19 improves — similar global research based on data from 132 countries found a positive correlation20.

Within companies, inclusive firms outperform their competitors. The more gender-diversity the executive team, the 25% more likely it is that a company's profitability is above average (McKinsey, 2020)21. Data from the stock market and other measures of long-term financial performance also suggest that LGBT-friendly companies are more competitive 22.

3.3.2 Diversity Fuels Innovation

Although early studies failed to provide clear evidence that homosexual people are more creative23, recent research paints a different picture. For instance, countries with larger LGBT populations tend to have higher Economic Complexity Indices (which measure a country's ability to innovate22). Research on psychological well-being also reveals that people expressing non-conventional gender or sexual identities tend to exhibit higher levels of cognitive flexibility— a trait highly correlated with creativity24. This could be one reason why innovative type societies are so friendly.

In the U.S., cities with large gay populations, like San Francisco, also have active tech scenes and sustained economic growth25. LGBT tech leaders like Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI show how in an inclusive environment, people can be encouraged to participate in innovation.

3.3.3 Attracting Talent in a Globalized Economy

In addition to innovation and productivity, gender inclusiveness is also important for attracting and retaining talent. Surveys indicate that young people are concerned about diversity and feeling safe at work. Countries and companies that promote gender diversity are more likely to attract highly skilled workers in this highly competitive global labor market.

4. Challenges

Although the importance of gender diversity is widely acknowledged, the following challenging issues still exist and need to be taken seriously.

4.1 Fairness in Sports

The participation of transgender women in competitive sports and, more recently, the participation of transgender women in boxing, as in Paris Olympics, have sparked great concern from people about the fairness of sports. People believe that there are still some physical advantages left after hormone treatment if this allows to participate in the sport, this causes a dilemma between being open and fair.

4.2 Reproduction and Evolution

The biggest problem that same-sex couples face is reproduction. Failure in reproduction is seen as a weakness from an evolutionary perspective. Although adoptions, surrogacies, and Assisted reproduction technology are available, they cannot reproduce through gamete fusion, so they can't pass on their gene. Yet, the number of same-sex marriages keeps going up instead of going down. Maybe that's a clue that biologic logic is not always consistent with social reality.

4.3 Gendered Spaces and Safety

The emergence of gender-neutral toilet benefits all but is it real safe? There are very few people pretend to be transexual to abuse the gender toilet. It reminds us that it is always hard to combine with the diversity and safety.

4.4 Risk of Exploitation

With the increase of social and economic capital of LGBT+ identity, there will be some people or groups to exploit it for their own interests. This phenomenon cover up the truth and fake the fact.

5. Solutions

Gender equality has brought obvious advantages to humanity, but it has also brought a host of related problems. We cannot go back to the times when women and men were clearly defined. Like Yu the Great who diverted floods not by building dams and walls to hold back the water but by digging channels and canals to let the water out, we should not cover up the truth.

The following principles may be useful:

• Questions of sports eligibility, reproductive ethics, and the like should be decided based on medically and biologically objective facts, not personal feelings.

• Anti-discrimination laws should be accompanied by laws that promote fairness and balance for everyone, not just minorities.

• Public education should be more than a hollow slogan but should help people to understand clearly what inclusivity and its effects can and cannot achieve.

• Both supporters and opponents of diversity should be aboveboard, so that no one can pull a fast one and there is less opportunity for misuse.

• One cannot talk of inclusion that is unconditional acceptance or indulgence, freedom and responsibility should go hand in hand; only by doing so could gender inclusion drive development in our rapidly growing society instead of hinders it.
